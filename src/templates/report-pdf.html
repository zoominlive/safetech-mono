3.0 ASSESSMENT METHODOLOGY

Assessment Approach:

The assessment was conducted using a systematic approach following industry best practices and regulatory requirements:

  1. Pre-Assessment Planning: Review of project scope, building plans, and historical documentation
  2. Visual Inspection: Comprehensive visual examination of all accessible areas
  3. Material Sampling: Collection of representative samples for laboratory analysis where required
  4. Documentation: Detailed documentation of findings with photographic evidence
  5. Risk Assessment: Evaluation of potential exposure risks during construction activities

Assessment Techniques:

The following assessment techniques were employed during this survey:

  • Visual Assessment: Systematic examination of building components and materials
  • Historical Review: Analysis of building age, construction methods, and renovation history
  • Material Sampling: Collection of bulk samples for laboratory analysis
  • Air Monitoring: Background air sampling where appropriate
  • Documentation: Comprehensive photographic and written documentation

Assessment Standards and Guidelines:

This assessment was conducted in accordance with the following standards and guidelines:

  • Ontario Regulation 278/05 - Designated Substance - Asbestos on Construction Projects and in Buildings and Repair Operations
  • Ontario Regulation 490/09 - Designated Substances
  • Canadian Standards Association (CSA) Z797-09 - Code of Practice for Access Scaffold
  • Workplace Safety and Insurance Board (WSIB) Guidelines for Asbestos Management
  • Ministry of Labour Guidelines for Designated Substances
  • Industry best practices for environmental assessment and hazardous materials management

Areas Assessed:

The assessment included systematic examination of all building areas including but not limited to:

  • Interior spaces and rooms
  • Mechanical and electrical rooms
  • Storage and utility areas
  • Ceiling and wall cavities
  • Building exterior and roofing systems
  • Underground and crawl spaces

Assessment Scope:

The assessment included evaluation of the following materials and conditions:

  • Asbestos-containing materials (ACM): Insulation, fireproofing, floor tiles, ceiling tiles, pipe insulation, and other building materials

Sampling and Analysis:

Where appropriate, representative samples were collected for laboratory analysis to confirm the presence and concentration of designated substances. Sampling was conducted following established protocols and chain of custody procedures to ensure sample integrity and analytical accuracy.

Laboratory analysis was performed by accredited laboratories using approved analytical methods. Results were interpreted in accordance with applicable regulations and guidelines to determine the significance of findings and appropriate control measures.

Quality Assurance:

This assessment was conducted by qualified environmental professionals with appropriate training and experience in designated substances assessment. All findings have been reviewed and verified to ensure accuracy and completeness.

Quality assurance measures included:

  • Review of assessment procedures and protocols
  • Verification of sampling and analytical methods
  • Cross-checking of findings and documentation
  • Peer review of assessment results and recommendations
  • Compliance verification with regulatory requirements

Limitations:

The assessment was limited to areas that were accessible at the time of inspection. Some areas may not have been accessible due to building conditions, safety considerations, or other constraints. The assessment results are based on conditions observed during the inspection period and may not reflect conditions that may develop or change over time.

Areas that were not accessible during the assessment should be evaluated prior to any construction activities to ensure comprehensive identification of all designated substances and hazardous materials.

Risk Assessment Summary:

Based on the findings of this assessment, the following risk factors have been identified:

  • Low Risk: Areas where no designated substances were identified or where materials are in good condition and unlikely to be disturbed
  • Medium Risk: Areas where designated substances are present but in good condition, requiring standard control measures during work activities
  • High Risk: Areas where designated substances are present in poor condition or where significant disturbance is anticipated during construction activities

Material Condition Assessment:

The condition of identified materials has been evaluated using standard industry criteria:

  • Good Condition: Materials are intact, undamaged, and unlikely to release fibers or particles under normal conditions
  • Fair Condition: Materials show minor damage or wear but are generally intact
  • Poor Condition: Materials are significantly damaged, deteriorated, or friable and may release fibers or particles

Exposure Assessment:

The potential for exposure to designated substances during construction activities has been evaluated based on the following factors:

  • Type and condition of materials present
  • Nature and extent of planned construction activities
  • Work methods and procedures to be employed
  • Environmental conditions and ventilation
  • Duration and frequency of work activities
  • Number of workers potentially exposed

Health Effects and Exposure Limits:

Exposure to designated substances can have various health effects depending on the substance, concentration, duration of exposure, and individual susceptibility. The following exposure limits and health effects are relevant to the materials identified in this assessment:

Asbestos:

Asbestos exposure can cause serious health effects including asbestosis, lung cancer, and mesothelioma. The Ontario exposure limit for asbestos is 0.1 fibers per cubic centimeter (f/cc) as an 8-hour time-weighted average. All work with asbestos-containing materials must be conducted in accordance with Regulation 278/05 requirements.

Lead:

Lead exposure can affect the nervous system, kidneys, and other organs. The Ontario exposure limit for lead is 0.05 milligrams per cubic meter (mg/m³) as an 8-hour time-weighted average. Lead-safe work practices must be implemented during renovation activities involving lead-containing materials.

Silica:

Crystalline silica exposure can cause silicosis, lung cancer, and other respiratory diseases. The Ontario exposure limit for respirable crystalline silica is 0.025 mg/m³ as an 8-hour time-weighted average. Appropriate dust control measures must be implemented during activities that may generate silica dust.

Mercury:

Mercury exposure can affect the nervous system, kidneys, and other organs. The Ontario exposure limit for mercury is 0.025 mg/m³ as an 8-hour time-weighted average. Special handling procedures are required for mercury-containing equipment.

Environmental Considerations:

In addition to worker health and safety, the assessment considered potential environmental impacts of designated substances. Proper disposal procedures must be followed for all hazardous materials to prevent environmental contamination and ensure compliance with environmental regulations.

Waste management procedures must be established for all designated substances and hazardous materials identified in this assessment. Disposal must be conducted at approved facilities in accordance with applicable regulations.

Specific Recommendations by Material Type:

Asbestos-Containing Materials:

  • Implement Type 1, 2, or 3 asbestos work procedures as appropriate
  • Ensure all workers receive appropriate asbestos awareness training
  • Use wet methods and HEPA filtration during removal activities
  • Implement proper containment and decontamination procedures
  • Arrange for proper disposal of asbestos waste at approved facilities

Lead-Containing Materials:

  • Implement lead-safe work practices during renovation activities
  • Use appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE)
  • Implement proper containment and cleanup procedures
  • Ensure proper disposal of lead-containing waste

Silica-Containing Materials:

  • Implement silica dust control measures during cutting and grinding activities
  • Use wet methods and local exhaust ventilation
  • Provide appropriate respiratory protection
  • Implement proper housekeeping procedures

Mercury-Containing Equipment:

  • Handle mercury-containing equipment with care to prevent breakage
  • Implement proper disposal procedures for mercury waste
  • Use appropriate PPE during handling and removal
  • Consider replacement with mercury-free alternatives

Training Requirements:

All workers involved in construction activities must receive appropriate training on:

  • Identification of designated substances and hazardous materials
  • Health effects and exposure risks
  • Safe work procedures and control measures
  • Proper use of personal protective equipment
  • Emergency procedures and incident response
  • Regulatory requirements and compliance obligations

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE):

The following personal protective equipment should be provided and used as appropriate:

  • Respiratory Protection: Appropriate respirators for the specific hazards encountered
  • Eye Protection: Safety glasses or goggles to prevent eye contact
  • Hand Protection: Gloves appropriate for the materials being handled
  • Body Protection: Coveralls or protective clothing as required
  • Foot Protection: Safety footwear appropriate for the work environment

Engineering Controls:

The following engineering controls should be implemented where appropriate:

  • Local exhaust ventilation systems
  • Wet methods for dust suppression
  • Work area isolation and containment
  • Negative pressure systems for asbestos work
  • HEPA filtration systems
  • Proper ventilation and air exchange

Administrative Controls:

The following administrative controls should be implemented:

  • Work scheduling to minimize exposure
  • Worker rotation to limit exposure duration
  • Access control to work areas
  • Signage and warning systems
  • Regular safety meetings and toolbox talks
  • Documentation and record keeping

Emergency Procedures:

In the event of unexpected discovery of hazardous materials or accidental release:

  1. Immediately stop work activities in the affected area
  2. Evacuate workers from the immediate area
  3. Notify the project supervisor and environmental consultant
  4. Implement appropriate containment measures
  5. Arrange for proper cleanup and disposal
  6. Document the incident and corrective actions taken

Waste Management:

Proper waste management procedures must be established for all designated substances and hazardous materials:

  • Segregation of different types of waste
  • Proper labeling and identification
  • Secure storage and containment
  • Transportation by licensed carriers
  • Disposal at approved facilities
  • Maintenance of disposal records and manifests

Regulatory Compliance:

All work activities must comply with applicable regulations including:

  • Ontario Occupational Health and Safety Act and regulations
  • Environmental Protection Act requirements
  • Municipal bylaws and building codes
  • Industry best practices and standards

Monitoring and Documentation:

Regular monitoring and documentation should be conducted throughout the project:

  • Air monitoring during work activities
  • Visual inspections of work areas
  • Documentation of control measure effectiveness
  • Worker health monitoring as required
  • Maintenance of detailed records
  • Regular review and update of procedures

Post-Construction Activities:

Following completion of construction activities, the following should be conducted:

  • Post-construction air monitoring
  • Visual inspection of completed work
  • Documentation of final conditions
  • Update of building records and documentation
